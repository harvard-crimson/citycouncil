The 2013 Cambridge City Council Elections

When Cambridge voters head to the polls on Tuesday, Nov. 5, they’ll pick from among 25 candidates, all of whom have different ideas about how best to negotiate University relations, fight crime, interact with the environment, legislate housing, and foster Square business in the city. Click on the tabs to examine the five women and 20 men vying to fill the Council’s nine seats: who they are and where they fall on the issues.

What they’re saying

One thing i would like to see happening is a program of robotics in our public schools supported by MIT.

As residents we are honored to live in the shadows of two of the greatest learning institutions in the world, but we cannot just live in their shadows. We can’t thrive as a city when we have two different populations, one wealthy and one poor. Close to 50% of the kids in the public school system live in near-poverty. We need to face these challenges.

There are inconveniences that come with the student population, so the least that the universities can do is giving back to the communities they live in. I think it shows good faith.

Dennis Benzan

I feel we have to keep increasing our police presence, especially in the squares. I would like to see a new police station in Central or Harvard.

My neighborhood in Cambridge, Area IV, was the first to adopt surveillance cameras in the 80s. We dealt with the cameras, but as a young person who was growing they felt uneasy. You felt your privacy was invaded.

Dennis Benzan

I support the net-zero movement and I support sustainability. However, I don’t support the current draft of the Connolly petition because it works against the goal of providing affordable housing.

A lot of pedestrians are incredibly unsafe around bikes. I propose that bikers should register their bikes and have a little plate on them, so that they may be identified. The elderly in particular have very serious complaints about bikes and how they go around the streets.

Dennis Benzan

We have to urge the universities to provide housing to the highest possible percentage of graduate students, so that the overflow won’t be putting extreme pressure on the neighborhoods’ housing markets.

The MBTA is underfunded. The Legislature has failed to prioritize it. We are a world-class city, and the fact that we have one of the worst transportation system in the country speaks to our priority. I would support making capital improvements. Whenever you jump on the red line it’s a gamble. You don’t know if you are going to get to work on time. It’s a sad state of affairs.

Dennis Benzan

I operate a small business, and I make a point every day to shop at local retail stores.

Dennis Benzan

Cambridge is endowed with an unbelievable range of great old buildings. I am a real believer that you should do everything you can to save them. I think most old buildings can be integrated into redevelopment.

Dennis Benzan

A practicing attorney and the son of Puerto Rican and Dominican immigrants, Benzan says that Cambridge is the city of dreams for him as much as it was for his parents. He says that he is committed to improving access to job training and industry for Cambridge residents.

Dennis Carlone

An architect and urban designer by profession, and a graduate of the Harvard Graduate School of Design (Master of Architecture in Urban Design, 1976), Carlone runs under the banner ‘planning for people.’ He says that one of his top priorities is to “create a city-wide, people-centered, comprehensive Master Plan that conforms with state law.”

Leland Cheung

Leland Cheung has held a seat on the City Council since 2009 and has been deeply committed to promoting affordable housing, government transparency and job growth since. While running in 2009 Cheung was pursuing a joint Masters in Public Administration at the Harvard Kennedy School of Government and MBA at the MIT Sloan School of Management--a program he is still enrolled in, making him the first currently enrolled student on the City Council. Cheung was also the first Asian American to be elected to the Cambridge City Council. In the last term Cheung has pursued his goals of increased transparency and growth plans by being Chair of the Cable TV, Telecommunications & Public Utilities Committee and the Neighborhood and Long Term Planning Committee.

Janneke House

The daughter of a policeman and schoolteacher, House learned early about the importance of public serivce. She now serves as the Director of Member and Community Relations for the Kendall Square Association (KSA), a nonprofit dedicated to improving, protecting and promoting Kendall Square. She has a Masters in Urban Planning and has put her background as a planner to work while she served as the first Executive Director of Cambridge Local First, another group dedicated to fostering a strong local economy for the city of Cambridge. A native of Salt Lake City, House has lived in Cambridge for a little over five years.

Craig Kelley

A seasoned veteran of the City Council, Craig A. Kelley is running for his fifth consecutive term on the Cambridge City Council and has chaired the Council’s Traffic, Parking and Transportation Committee, the Public Safety Cubcomittee and Veteran’s Committee. Although not a complete Cambridge native, Kelley was born and raised in close-by Wellesley and later attended Boston College Law School where he was awarded the Susan B. Desmaris award for Public Service Achievement and Leadership for his work on environmetnal issues at the school. Kelley and his wife, Hope, can’t imagine raising their two children anywhere outside Cambridge and beyond his chair positions Kelley is regularly involved in everything from the licensing committee to the school committe to keep Cambridge the vibrant place it is.

James Lee

Originally from South Korea, and a graduate of Harvard’s Graduate School of Arts and Sciences (PhD, 2003), Lee became a naturalized U.S. citizen in September 2011. Lee has been a resident of Cambridge for 14 years, where he has worked in education and in the financial services industry. “I think there is a need for someone like me on the Council,” Lee says. “Someone who is a brand-new American and a naturalized US citizen.”

Logan Leslie

Harvard sophomore and former Green Beret, Logan E. Leslie is running on the promise of bridging Cambridge’s town-gown divide. If elected, Leslie said, he would represent the student voice on the Council, establishing and soliciting advice from a committee that of unversity students. If he wins a seat on the Council this November, Leslie will be the first Harvard College student to serve on the Council in recent history.

David Maher

A Cambridge native and former Harvard Square Coop store manager, David P. Maher brings a wealth of business and non-profit experience to the City Council. His priority as candidate is to use his experience as former Mayor and serving seven terms as City Councillor, and four terms on the School Committe, toimprove and deepen his involvement with quality of life issues in neighborhoods.

Nadeem Mazen

Nadeem Mazen, an Independent and life-long resident of the Greater Boston area, aims to bring his experience as a small business-owner and educator to the Cambridge City Council. His priority as a candidate is to use self-imposed term limits to ensure that the new voices frequently enter the Council. He said he will work to achieve “consensus-based, city-first not me-first politicians, who are willing to work for generational change” and reverse the current trend of city leadership that remains in power for upwards of 20 years.

Marc McGovern

A fourth generation resident of Cambridge and father of four, McGovern has lived in Cambridge his whole life. Marc has served four terms on the Cambridge School Committee. During his tenure, he has championed early childhood education. He began his career a social worker working with special needs children in foster care and now says that he hopes to bring the systematic, common-sense approach of social work to decision-making on the Council.

Gary Mello

Democrat Gary Mello has lived in Cambridge his whole life and said he is primarily running in this year’s election because he is “disturbed by the way the city spends money” and is “worried about the long-term effects of our largesse.” Mello plans to find ways to reduce overall city spending, but also to prioritize spending on public healthcare.

Mushtaque Mirza

Democratic candidate Mushtaque Mirza is looking to fill a seat on the City Council in the city where the native of India has lived for the past 40 years. Mirza believes “the candidate belongs to everybody.” The environmental engineer said that after travelling to Antarctica and witnessing firsthand the effects of climate change, he plans to prioritize the improvement of Cambridge’s environment policies and increased access to affordable housing.

Gregg Moree

Couldn’t be reached.

Ron Peden

Ronald Peden has lived in Cambridge for fifteen years. Originally from Philadelphia, he’s running for city council for the first time this year and hopes to put his own ideas for change forward after working for years as an activist. Peden would like to see Harvard engage more substantially in the support of local businesses, a greater rapport between the police and the Cambridge community, closing the wealth and income gap that exists within Cambridge, and pursuing a development strategy that takes into account all competing interests within Cambridge.

Lesley Phillips

Lesley Phillips, originally from New York City, has lived in Cambridge for 31 years. This is her first time running for Cambridge City Council. Phillips is focused on increasing student participation in the city’s political process, improving public safety via improving public transportation, continuing and expanding Cambridge’s commitment to sustainable energy, encouraging small, locally owned businesses to flourish in Cambridge, and striking an equitable balance between residential housing and urban development.

Ken Reeves

Kenneth Reeves has held a seat on the City Council since 1989, serving as mayor from 1992-1995 and then again from 2006-2007. He was the first openly-gay black mayor in the United States.

Sam Seidel

Seidel graduated from the Graduate School of Design with a degree in Urban Planning in 2011. He served on the city council between 2007 and 2011. During that period, Seidel served as the vice-mayor during 2010 and chaired the Ordinance Committee and the Neighborhood and Long Term Planning Committee. He lost a reelection campaign and subsequently travelled in France for several months before coming back to Cambridge with a renewed drive and new ideas about how to improve the Council. He has run on a platform that revolves around education, the environment and urban planning.

Denise Simmons

E. Denise Simmons has held a seat on the City Council since 2001, serving as mayor from 2008-2009. She was the first openly-lesbian black mayor in the United States.

Jefferson Smith

Democratic candidate Jefferson R. Smith pushes a progressive agenda advocating development, transportation, and sustainability reforms. Smith was born in Lawrence, Massachusetts, and moved to Cambridge ten years ago to work with State Representative Tim Toomey’s public safety and security committee. Later, as legislative director for MassDOT under Gov. Deval Patrick, Smith helped draft the governor’s Way Forward Plan, which brought in about six million for transportation in the commonwealth. He has also worked with Mass Recycle and the Cambridge Democrats. In this election, Smith says that his main issue will be housing affordability, specifically making rents more affordable for young professionals and new families. Smith is running for the position of city councilman for the first time this fall.

Tim Toomey

Timothy J. Toomey Jr. has held a seat on the City Council since 1990 and has served as Massachussettes State Representative since 1993. He is a lifelong resident of East Cambridge and an avid supporter of affordable housing, job creation, and sound fiscal management.

Minka VanBeuzekorn

vanBeuzekom has served on the Cambridge city council since 2012. Although she originally hails from New Jersey, vanBeuzekom has lived in Cambridge for 33 years -- ever since she graduated from Wellsley College. During her tenure as councilwoman, she has spearheaded environments proposals such as introducing an ombudsman to coordinate the introduction of solar pannels into Cambridge. On the Council, she now chairs the Environment and the Transportation Committee and Traffic & Parking Committee. If reelected, she hopes to continue to bring good listening skills and transparency -- qualities that, she says, the Council often lacks.

Luis Vasquez

Luis Vasquez, 25, describes himself as an "activist of the youthful go-getter generation." He graduated from Cambridge Rindge and Latin High School in 2006, and his plans to go to then pursue higher education were halted when he became a father at the age of 19. The experience of being a teenage father, he says, has inspired him to "guiding young people" and serving the Cambridge community. Vasquez has been a lifelong Cantabrigian, and is the son of Latin American immigrants. He identifies his political priorities as providing greater opportunities for affordable living in Cambridge, aiding non-profit organizations, providing a support network for the graduates of Cambridge public schools, and encouraging health and fitness among Cambridge’s youth.

Kirsten Von Hoffmann

Currently Sustainability Manager for the Cambridge public school system, Kristen von Hoffman, 29, is running for City Council in hopes of attending to a wide range of municipal issues that includes but is not limited to education and environmental sustainability. If elected, she hopes to work on bringing universal pre-K to Cambridge, creating a comprehensive sustainability plan for the city, and increasing the number of family-friendly affordable housing units.

James Williamson

Being a Cambridge resident for the past 42 years, James Williamson takes pride in his knowledge of the city. If elected to City Council, one of the main concerns that he intends to focus on is a growing commercialism in Harvard Square. Williamson intends to support local business growth by making it easier for small businesses to acquire permits. He also is interesting in finding ways to communicate with both landlords and small business owners to keep Cambridge rents down. Williamson wants to encourage MIT and Harvard to house all University graduate students. Opening up more housing for non-students would allow for more space dedicated to public and subsidized housing, as well as lower overall rents as the availability of apartments increases. Williamson is also interested in raising the percentage (currently around 12%) of housing units allocated to affordable housing. Williamson intends to work with Harvard and Drew Faust to possibly renegotiate Harvard’s Payments in Lieu of Taxes (PILOT) to the city of Cambridge. He believes that Harvard owes the city of Cambridge more attention, especially on the forefront of the Allston campus construction and the traffic that will create. Williamson is in favor of increased communication between the Cambridge City Council and all local Universities, especially Harvard. Regarding local crime, Williamson supports the idea of increasing community police and strengthening the relationship between HUPD and Cambridge Police instead of strengthening punishment and surveillance measures.

Elie is running because he believes that the democratic government should be restored to Cambridge, as to produce a government that is open to the citizens of the city.
